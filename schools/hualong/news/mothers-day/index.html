Mother's Day · 母親節

A Letter to Mother

寄乎阿母的批
May 6, 2026 · School-wide ceremony · 全校性活動 · Three languages, one hall · 三語並列、一個禮堂

Ocarina, carnations, "thank-you socks," and a principal with a guitar — on Mother's Day at Hualong, gratitude was sung in three languages. 陶笛、康乃馨、感恩襪，和一位抱著吉他的校長——華龍國小的母親節，把「謝謝」用三種語言唱了出來。

The opening notes 陶笛揭開序幕

Five o'clock the day before Mother's Day, the assembly hall began to fill. The ceremony opened with the small breath of the ocarina — a clay flute that fits in a child's palm — carrying a song every Taiwanese mother knows: Mothers of the World Are All the Same.

The hall went still. Mothers who had hurried in from work, grandmothers who had walked from across the road, fathers standing at the back — everyone listened. The song is older than most of the children playing it, but the children played it anyway, the way you might pass on a quiet family secret.

母親節前夕，禮堂逐漸坐滿。活動由悠揚的陶笛聲揭開序曲——學生們用心吹奏著家喻戶曉的《天下的媽媽都是一樣的》。

整個禮堂安靜下來。從工作中趕來的媽媽、從巷口走來的阿嬤、站在後排的爸爸——所有人都在聽。這首歌比演奏的孩子們年紀還大，但孩子們依然認真地演奏著，像在傳遞一個安靜的家族秘密。

Cards, carnations, "thank-you socks" 卡片、康乃馨，與感恩襪

Then came the gifts. Each child carried up three things: a handmade card, a single carnation, and a pair of socks — what the school called "thank-you socks," chosen because socks are the kind of practical, daily gift a child can imagine their mother actually wearing.

The detail mattered. We didn't want gratitude to feel ceremonial. We wanted it to feel useful — something Mom puts on next Wednesday morning when she's running late, and somewhere underneath the rush, remembers her child made this happen.

接著是禮物。每個孩子親手致贈家長三樣東西：手工卡片、一朵康乃馨，和一雙襪子——學校稱為「感恩襪」，因為襪子是孩子真的能想像媽媽會穿的、實用的、日常的禮物。

這個細節很重要。我們不想讓感恩變成儀式，而想讓它變成有用的東西——下個禮拜三早上媽媽匆忙穿襪子的時候，在那份忙碌底下，會想起：這雙襪子，是孩子親手選給她的。

Loving home, May 15 愛家 515：國際家庭日

Halfway through the ceremony, our director took the microphone to mention something that often goes unnoticed in Taiwan: the United Nations' International Day of Families, May 15. The Taiwanese campaign around it has a memorable handle — 愛家 515, "Love Your Family 515."

Its message is small and specific: use your five senses — eyes, ears, mouth, hands, and heart — to close the distance with the people you live with. Look at them. Listen to them. Speak kindly to them. Touch them. Mean it.

Mother's Day, in this framing, is one moment in a longer practice. The school wanted that connection drawn explicitly — gratitude as a habit, not a holiday.

活動中段，主任接過麥克風，藉機宣導一件台灣常被忽略的事：聯合國訂定的國際家庭日，每年 5 月 15 日。台灣社群的口號是 愛家 515。

它的訊息很小、很具體：用你的眼、耳、口、手、心五到，去縮短和家人之間的距離。看著他們、聽他們、好好對他們說話、觸碰他們、發自內心。

在這個脈絡下，母親節只是一個更長的練習中的一個瞬間。學校希望把這個連結說清楚——感恩，是一個習慣，不是一個節日。

The principal closed with his guitar, singing a Hokkien classic called ‘A Letter to Mother.' The whole hall went quiet. 活動的高潮，是校長的深情吉他獻唱〈寄乎阿母的批〉，深刻觸動了在座師生與家長的心弦。

The principal's guitar 校長的吉他

The closing song was unannounced. Principal Hong Wei-zhen, who had taken office just nine months earlier, walked up with a guitar he had quietly brought from home. He tuned it, smiled at the families, and began to sing — in Hokkien (Taiwanese), the language of most of the grandmothers in the room.

The song was Kià hōo a-bú ê phue — A Letter to Mother. A son writes to his mother from far away. He apologizes for not visiting often enough. He thanks her for the meals he didn't notice she was preparing for him. He hopes she is sleeping well. He signs it with the kind of restraint that makes Hokkien songs hurt.

By the second verse, mothers in the hall had stopped trying to hide their tears. Children glanced sideways. A grandmother in the front row reached for her daughter's hand. The principal kept singing.

結尾的歌沒有事前預告。剛上任九個月的洪維振校長，帶著一把從家裡靜靜搬來的吉他走上台。他調音，朝家長們微笑，然後開始唱——用台語，用禮堂裡大多數阿嬤聽得懂的語言。

那首歌叫〈寄乎阿母的批〉。一個兒子從遠方寫信給媽媽。他為自己回家不夠頻繁道歉。他感謝那些媽媽默默為他準備、他卻沒注意的飯。他希望她睡得好。他用台語歌特有的、那種會讓人心痛的克制，把信收尾。

唱到第二段時，禮堂裡的媽媽們已經不再隱藏眼淚。孩子們偷偷看向一旁。前排一位阿嬤伸手握住女兒的手。校長繼續唱。

Why three languages mattered 為什麼三種語言會重要

One ceremony, three languages: ocarina-played Mandarin, official Mandarin from the stage, and Hokkien sung by the principal. We did not choose this consciously — it just happened, the way a real Taiwan classroom always sounds anyway. But it mattered.

For the children, watching their principal sing in Hokkien is a small permission slip. Hokkien isn't only the language of their grandparents — it's the language of the school they go to every day. For the grandmothers, having the song sung in their language is something more than nostalgia: it's a sign that the school sees them.

"Let love continue, together build a beautiful family," the post on the school's Facebook page closed. 讓愛能夠延續，共創美好家庭。 We meant it across all three languages.

一場活動，三種語言：陶笛吹奏的國語歌、台上致詞的國語、校長獻唱的台語。我們沒有刻意安排——這只是台灣真實教室本來就有的聲音。但這件事很重要。

對孩子來說，看著自己的校長用台語唱歌，是一張小小的許可證——台語不只是阿公阿嬤的語言，也是他們每天上學那所學校的語言。對阿嬤來說，能聽到一首歌用她們的語言唱出來，比懷舊更有意義：這是一個訊號——學校看見她們。

「讓愛能夠延續，共創美好家庭。」學校 FB 粉專那一則貼文是這樣收尾的。我們是真心的——這句話對三種語言的人，都成立。
