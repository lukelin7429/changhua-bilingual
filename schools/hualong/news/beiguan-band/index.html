The Fifth-Grade Beiguan Band

五年級的小小絲竹團
March 27 — April 4, 2026 · A two-act story · 雙篇敘事 · Two lessons · 兩堂課

One Tuesday lunchtime, a group of fifth-graders walked into the music room and told their teacher: "We want to play at the Children's Day class show." Two lessons later, they had assembled a small ensemble — and they were quietly practicing on their own. 某個禮拜二中午，幾個五年級的孩子走進音樂教室告訴老師：「我們要在兒童節班級活動上表演。」兩堂課後，他們組成了一個小小絲竹團——而且，他們私底下肯定有自行加練。

What is Beiguan, anyway? 什麼是北管？

Before the story, a quick orientation. Beiguan (北管, literally "northern strings and pipes") is a traditional Taiwanese folk music form, brought across the strait in the 17th and 18th centuries and shaped over generations into something distinctly Taiwanese. You hear it most often at temple festivals, weddings, and parades — loud, bright, and proudly local. The Taiwan government recognizes it as national intangible cultural heritage.

Within Beiguan are two main divisions: a louder, percussion-and-wind ensemble (鼓吹樂) you hear in temple processions, and a quieter, string-led ensemble (絲竹樂) more suited to indoor performance. The latter — "silk and bamboo" — is what our fifth-graders chose.

故事之前，先做一個簡單的介紹。北管（字面意思是「北方的絃和管」）是台灣的傳統民間音樂，在 17、18 世紀渡海而來，經過幾個世代，演化成具有鮮明台灣特色的音樂形式。你最常在廟會、婚禮、遊行陣頭聽到它——響亮、明亮、驕傲地在地。政府將北管列為國家無形文化資產。

北管裡有兩大分支：以打擊與管樂為主、聲音較大的「鼓吹樂」，常見於廟會陣頭；以及以絃樂為主、較適合室內演出的「絲竹樂」。後者——「絲和竹」——就是我們五年級孩子選擇的形式。

I
Act One · Practice
第一幕 · 練習

Tuesday lunchtime, the music room 週二中午，音樂教室

Every Tuesday at lunch, the Beiguan club's silk-and-bamboo strings come alive in the music room. Teachers split the children into small groups by skill level — beginners with one teacher, intermediates with another — and adapt the lessons on the fly: a different score here, an easier finger position there. It is, in the most ordinary way, just music class.

What was different this spring was that a group of fifth-graders walked in on the first week of school and told their teacher: "We want to play at the Children's Day class show. Help us."

The teacher said yes — with the careful skepticism teachers reserve for ambitious children. Two weeks. Two lessons. A traditional Beiguan score called Spring Strings (春串). It was not, on paper, enough time.

每週二中午，北管社團孩子們的絲弦樂器在音樂教室裡甦醒。老師把孩子依程度分成小組——新手跟一位老師、中階跟另一位老師——並依教學狀況臨機調整教材：這裡換一張譜，那裡簡化一個指法。它，在最平凡的意義上，就是一堂音樂課。

但這個春天不一樣。剛開學第一週，幾個五年級的孩子走進來告訴老師：「我們要在兒童節的班級活動表演，幫我們！」

老師答應了——帶著老師對野心勃勃的孩子才會有的、那種小心翼翼的懷疑。兩週時間。兩堂課。一首傳統北管絃譜，叫〈春串〉。在紙上來看，這時間是不夠的。

Two lessons later, they had assembled a small ensemble. They were, the teacher noted, quietly practicing on their own. 短短兩次上課就組成了一個小小絲竹團——老師注意到，他們私底下肯定有自行加練。
II
Act Two · Performance
第二幕 · 演出

Children's Day, on stage 兒童節，舞台上

By Children's Day, the band could play Spring Strings from beginning to end. Not perfectly — the bowing was a little uneven, the tempo wandered — but cleanly enough that adults who knew the piece could recognize it, and adults who didn't know it could feel the spring in it.

Most of the Beiguan we tend to picture in our heads is loud — gongs, suonas, marching feet. This wasn't that. Five children, sitting upright, fingers on strings, breathing together. A "very elegant Band," as the school's Facebook post described them, with affectionate quotation marks.

The applause came not because they had played a difficult piece (they had), but because they had built the whole thing themselves — from the first conversation with the teacher to the last note on stage. The teachers' job had been to stay out of the way at the right moments.

到了兒童節，這個小小絲竹團已經能從頭到尾演奏完〈春串〉。不是完美——運弓有點不齊、速度會跑——但夠乾淨，懂這首曲子的大人能認出來，不懂的大人也能感覺到春天在裡面。

我們腦中對北管的印象通常是大聲的——鑼、嗩吶、踏步。這次不是。五個孩子坐得很直，手指放在弦上，一起呼吸。學校 FB 粉專親切地用引號形容他們是「很文雅的 Band」。

掌聲響起來，不是因為他們演奏了一首難曲（他們的確演奏了），而是因為這整件事都是他們自己組起來的——從跟老師的第一次對話，到舞台上最後一個音符。老師們做的事，是在對的時刻退到一旁。

A small glossary 小小詞彙表

Three Beiguan terms that came up in this story, for readers new to Taiwanese folk music:

Beiguan běi-guǎn 北管
A 300-year-old Taiwanese folk music tradition, brought from southern China and developed locally. Listed as national intangible cultural heritage. 擁有 300 年歷史的台灣傳統民間音樂，源自中國南方，在地化發展。列為國家無形文化資產。
Silk-and-bamboo ensemble sī-zhú-tuán 絲竹團
The quieter Beiguan branch, built around string and bamboo-wind instruments. "Silk" refers to historical strings spun from silk; "bamboo" refers to flutes. Suited to indoor, intimate playing. 北管中較安靜的分支，以絲弦樂器與竹管樂器為主。「絲」指古代用蠶絲製成的弦；「竹」指竹笛。適合室內演奏。
Spring Strings chūn-chuàn 春串
A traditional Beiguan score celebrating the arrival of spring. The piece carries a hopeful, bright melodic line built for the silk-and-bamboo ensemble. 慶祝春日到來的傳統北管絃譜，旋律明亮、充滿希望，為絲竹團而作。

Why this story matters 為什麼這個故事重要

It would have been a much shorter story if the school had simply assigned the band. Or if a teacher had just selected an easier, more "kid-friendly" arrangement. Or if the goal had been to win something.

None of that is what happened. A group of children walked into a music room and asked permission to attempt something that, on paper, was probably too hard. The teachers said yes. The children went home and practiced on their own. Two weeks later, they finished what they had started.

That sequence — ask, get a yes, do the work yourself — is something a small school can offer that a big school often cannot. There is room here for a child's idea to take up the entire stage. We try to keep it that way.

如果學校直接派這個樂團，故事會短很多。或者老師直接挑一首比較簡單、比較「適合小孩」的編曲。或者目標是去比賽得獎。

這些都不是真的發生的事。一群孩子走進音樂教室，要求嘗試一件紙上看來可能太難的事。老師答應了。孩子回家自己加練。兩週後，他們把開始的事完成了。

這個順序——提問、得到允許、自己把事做完——是小學校可以給的禮物，大學校往往做不到。在這裡，一個孩子的想法可以佔滿整個舞台。我們努力讓它一直是這樣。
